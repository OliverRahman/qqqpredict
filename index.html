How it works ▾

The short version

Every day a computer program looks at how the stock market has been behaving lately and makes one guess: will the Nasdaq-100 (the QQQ fund, basically the 100 biggest tech-ish companies) close higher or lower tomorrow? Then it tells you how sure it is.

What it actually looks at

It watches roughly 30 signals, things like recent price moves, how jumpy the market's been (volatility), trading volume, and the VIX "fear gauge." The single most useful signal turns out to be how scared the market is, not where prices are.

How it learns

It studies several years of past market days, learns which patterns tended to come before up-days versus down-days, and then applies that to today. It's a fairly simple, deliberately un-fancy model. Fancier ones mostly just memorized noise and did worse.

Why it only claims ~53%

Predicting the market day-to-day is genuinely close to a coin flip, and anyone claiming way more than this is usually fooling themselves. A real edge here is small. The honest goal isn't "always right," it's "right slightly more than half the time, with the losses kept small."

Built and run by one person as a research project, still being tested in real time. It hasn't been proven with real money, doesn't account for trading costs, and is shown here purely out of curiosity. Not advice, just a robot's daily guess.
